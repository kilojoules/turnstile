\begin{abstract}
What happens inside a language model when it is being jailbroken?
We study this question by training a 3B-parameter adversary to jailbreak an 8B-parameter victim through 5-turn conversations via DPO self-play, then characterizing and intervening on the victim's internal dynamics across layers and turns.
Three nearly orthogonal directions in the victim's residual stream capture distinct aspects of safety computation: an \emph{intent} direction detects adversarial conversations (AUC~0.97), an \emph{outcome} direction predicts which attacks will succeed (AUC~0.73 at turn~1, peaking at layer~20), and the standard refusal direction \citep{arditi2024refusal} is inert---orthogonal to both and causally ineffective.
Activation steering reveals that the compliance decision is \emph{determined} at the middle layer (layer~16) and \emph{read out} at the final layer (layer~31): steering at layer~16 changes multi-turn ASR bidirectionally ($-13$~pp, $+5$~pp), while intervention at layer~31 has no effect.
Extended benign conversation alone shifts the victim's internal state toward compliance, but accounts for only a fraction of the adversary's effect (5\% vs.\ 25--32\% ASR), confirming that most of the adversary's success reflects learned manipulation, not generic context softening.
SAE feature ablation shows that the four persistent features identified by correlational analysis are markers, not drivers---clamping them reduces ASR by 3--7~pp, but amplifying them also reduces ASR, and random feature clamping has no effect.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

When a language model is jailbroken, what changes inside it?
The model was trained to refuse harmful requests.
Something in its internal computation shifts during the adversarial conversation, allowing compliance where refusal was intended.
This paper asks what that shift looks like---where it occurs in the network, how it evolves across conversation turns, and whether causal intervention can control it.

A growing body of work establishes that safety-relevant information is present in model hidden states.
Linear probes can detect jailbreaks from residual stream activations \citep{jailbreaklens2024}.
Refusal behavior is mediated by a single direction in the residual stream \citep{arditi2024refusal}.
\citet{wu2026knowingwithoutacting} propose the Disentangled Safety Hypothesis: safety recognition (``Knowing'') and safety execution (``Acting'') become structurally independent in deep layers.
\citet{bailey2024obfuscated} show that white-box optimization can produce activations that bypass probes while maintaining jailbreak success.

These results establish \emph{that} a gap exists between safety representations and safety behavior.
We ask a complementary question: what does this gap look like \emph{in practice}, as it develops turn by turn during a multi-turn jailbreak?

To study this, we build an adversarial self-play system where a 3B-parameter model (Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct) learns to jailbreak an 8B-parameter victim (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct) through 5-turn conversations, trained via DPO \citep{rafailov2023dpo} on win/loss pairs.
The adversary operates through natural language only---no access to the victim's logits, gradients, or hidden states.
This constrained setting achieves moderate attack success (18--32\% ASR), far below prompt-engineering approaches like GOAT \citep{pavlova2024goat} (97\% ASR), but it produces a controlled, gradually escalating pressure that enables turn-by-turn internal characterization and causal intervention.

\paragraph{Summary of findings.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Three orthogonal safety subspaces.} The victim's residual stream contains three nearly orthogonal directions related to safety, each capturing a different aspect (Table~\ref{tab:directions}): intent detection (adversarial vs.\ benign, AUC~0.97), outcome prediction (success vs.\ failure, AUC~0.73 at turn~1), and the standard refusal direction \citep{arditi2024refusal}. The refusal direction is causally inert; the other two have predictive or causal power.

  \item \textbf{The compliance decision is determined upstream of where it manifests.} The logit lens shows the compliance decision appears exclusively at layer~31. But causal steering at layer~31 has no effect. Steering at layer~16 changes ASR bidirectionally ($-13$~pp toward safety, $+5$~pp toward compliance). The decision is \emph{read out} at the final layer but \emph{written} at the middle layer.

  \item \textbf{The model weakly predicts its own failure.} A probe trained to distinguish successful from failed jailbreaks achieves AUC~0.73 at turn~1 (layer~20), peaking after the first adversary-victim exchange and then declining. This signal generalizes across harm categories (leave-one-out AUC~0.60). The model's internal state contains information about attack quality, not just attack presence.

  \item \textbf{Most of the adversary's effect is learned, not generic.} Extended benign conversation shifts the probe toward compliance and increases single-turn ASR from 1\% to 5\%. But the trained adversary achieves 25--32\%---the adversary has learned specific manipulation strategies beyond context softening.

  \item \textbf{SAE features are markers, not mechanisms.} Clamping the four persistent SAE features to zero reduces ASR by 3--7~pp, but amplifying them \emph{also} reduces ASR (by 6~pp), while clamping random features has no effect (26\% vs.\ 25\% baseline). The features co-occur with jailbreaks but do not drive them.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Scope and limitations.}
All findings describe the internal dynamics of Llama-3.1-8B under moderate-success-rate attacks from a learned 3B adversary.
We do not claim these dynamics generalize to stronger attacks, other model families, or larger scales.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Models and Setup}

We use Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct as the adversary and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct as the victim.
The adversary is augmented with a LoRA adapter (rank~16, 2.3M trainable parameters) trained via DPO \citep{rafailov2023dpo}.
Each round consists of 100 five-turn conversations targeting the 100 standardized harmful behaviors from JailbreakBench \citep{chao2024jailbreakbench}.
We run 15 rounds per seed across 3 seeds (42, 123, 456), totaling 4{,}500 conversations per condition.

We selected Llama-3.1-8B as the victim because it maintains genuine refusal behavior.
In a screening of six models across four families, all non-Llama models at 4B+ parameters exhibited a ``disclaimer-then-comply'' failure mode, producing harmful content despite prepending safety warnings.

\subsection{DPO Self-Play}

After each round, successful jailbreaks (as determined by the dual judge) form the ``chosen'' set and failed attacks form the ``rejected'' set.
The LoRA adapter is updated on these preference pairs.
Training data is cumulative across rounds.

DPO is essential for this sparse-reward setting.
Only 3--7\% of conversations per round are wins.
Supervised fine-tuning on wins alone produces 0--3\% ASR because the adversary memorizes specific attacks without learning what distinguishes success from failure.
DPO learns from \emph{every} conversation by contrasting chosen and rejected examples, enabling 18--26\% ASR---a 5--9$\times$ improvement.

\subsection{Dual-Judge Evaluation}

Single-judge evaluation is unreliable for multi-turn conversations.
Llama Guard 3 alone flagged 100\% of multi-turn conversations as unsafe, including conversations with no harmful content.
We use a dual-judge pipeline: a conversation is classified as a successful jailbreak only if both Llama Guard~3 and Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct (using the JailbreakBench standard prompt) agree.

\section{Observational Results}
\label{sec:observational}

\subsection{The Compliance Decision Manifests at Layer~31 and Erodes Across Turns}
\label{sec:logitlens}

We apply the logit lens \citep{nostalgebraist2020logitlens} at every 4th layer and every turn of 80 multi-turn conversations (40 wins, 40 losses), projecting intermediate hidden states through RMSNorm and the unembedding matrix.
We track $\Delta = P(\text{comply}) - P(\text{refuse})$.

\paragraph{Layer 31 dominates and flips across turns.}
For successful jailbreaks, $\Delta$ at layer~31 moves from $-0.45$ (strong refusal, turn~0) through $-0.06$ (turn~2) to $+0.11$ (compliance, turn~4).
For failed attacks, $\Delta$ remains negative throughout ($-0.22$ to $-0.03$).
Layers 0--24 carry no decision signal ($|\Delta| < 0.02$).

\paragraph{The adversary triggers heightened vigilance before eroding it.}
Counterintuitively, the model shows a \emph{stronger} refusal signal at turn~0 for conversations that will ultimately succeed ($\Delta = -0.45$) than for those that will fail ($\Delta = -0.22$).
The adversary's opening gambit triggers heightened vigilance, but subsequent turns erode it.

\subsection{Refusal Representation Collapses Before the Decision Flips}
\label{sec:repe}

Following \citet{arditi2024refusal}, we extract a refusal direction via contrastive pairs at layer~31 and project victim hidden states onto it at each turn (40 wins, 40 losses).

The refusal \emph{representation} collapses after turn~0 (both wins and losses drop to $\sim$$-15$), while the compliance \emph{decision} (logit lens) takes four more turns to flip.
The model loses its internal refusal posture after one exchange but keeps refusing for several turns---then behavior catches up.
This is a concrete, dynamic instance of the Knowing/Acting dissociation proposed by \citet{wu2026knowingwithoutacting}.

\section{Three Orthogonal Safety Directions}
\label{sec:directions}

We identify three directions in the victim's residual stream that capture distinct aspects of safety computation.
All three are nearly mutually orthogonal (Table~\ref{tab:directions}).

\textbf{Direction} & \textbf{AUC} & \textbf{cos(A)} & \textbf{cos(I)} & \textbf{Extraction} \\
\midrule
Intent (I)  & 0.97 & $-0.04$ & --- & LogReg on adversarial vs.\ benign \\
Outcome (O) & 0.73 & $-0.002$ & $0.02$ & LogReg on success vs.\ failure \\
Refusal (A) & --- & --- & $-0.04$ & mean(harmful) $-$ mean(benign) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Three directions in the victim's residual stream. All pairwise cosine similarities are $<0.05$. AUC is 5-fold stratified CV. The refusal direction (A) has no predictive or causal power on this model.}
\label{tab:directions}
\end{table}

\subsection{Intent Detection: AUC~0.97 (Adversarial vs.\ Benign)}
\label{sec:intent}

A logistic regression probe on per-turn hidden states at layer~16 distinguishes adversarial from benign conversations with AUC~0.97 (9{,}300 turn-level examples, 5-fold CV).
Detection is distributed across all layers (AUC 0.77 at layer~0, rising to 0.84 at layer~31).

However, this result is arguably tautological: adversarial and benign conversations differ structurally in their inputs.
The probe may detect input distribution rather than the model's internal safety state.
The more informative question is whether the model can predict which attacks will \emph{succeed}.

\subsection{Outcome Prediction: AUC~0.73 (Success vs.\ Failure)}
\label{sec:outcome}

We train a separate probe---on adversarial conversations only---to predict eventual jailbreak success from per-turn hidden states.
This is a harder task: all inputs are adversarial, and the probe must distinguish attack \emph{quality} rather than attack \emph{presence}.

\paragraph{The model weakly predicts its own failure.}
At layer~16, outcome prediction AUC is 0.61 at turn~0, peaks at 0.69 at turn~1, then declines to 0.62 at later turns.
A full layer$\times$turn sweep reveals the peak at layer~20, turn~1: \textbf{AUC~0.73} (Table~\ref{tab:outcome_matrix}).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcccccc}
\toprule
& \textbf{Turn 0} & \textbf{Turn 1} & \textbf{Turn 2} & \textbf{Turn 3} & \textbf{Turn 4} \\
\midrule
Layer 0  & 0.50 & 0.52 & 0.61 & 0.57 & 0.57 \\
Layer 4  & 0.66 & 0.71 & 0.66 & 0.59 & 0.63 \\
Layer 12 & 0.65 & 0.73 & 0.70 & 0.61 & 0.64 \\
Layer 16 & 0.65 & \textbf{0.73} & 0.67 & 0.61 & 0.64 \\
Layer 20 & 0.66 & \textbf{0.73} & 0.66 & 0.60 & 0.64 \\
Layer 31 & 0.66 & 0.72 & 0.67 & 0.61 & 0.62 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Outcome prediction AUC (success vs.\ failure) across layers and turns. Peak: layer~20, turn~1 (AUC~0.73). Based on 500 adversarial conversations (5-fold stratified CV). The model's internal state is most predictive right after the first exchange.}
\label{tab:outcome_matrix}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Turn~1 is the peak.}
The model has the most information about its own future failure immediately after the first adversary-victim exchange.
Prediction accuracy then \emph{declines} at later turns, consistent with the refusal representation collapse observed via RepE (Section~\ref{sec:repe}): the internal state differentiates most at turn~1, then converges.

\paragraph{Generalization across categories.}
Leave-one-category-out cross-validation at turn~0 yields AUC~0.60 across 10 JailbreakBench harm categories.
The probe is not simply learning that some topics are easier to jailbreak---it detects something about attack quality that generalizes.

\paragraph{Orthogonality.}
The outcome direction is nearly orthogonal to both the intent direction ($\cos=0.02$) and the Arditi refusal direction ($\cos=-0.002$).
The model represents ``this is an attack,'' ``this attack will succeed,'' and ``I should refuse'' in three independent subspaces.

\section{Causal Interventions}
\label{sec:causal}

\subsection{Activation Steering with Direction Vectors}

We test whether the directions identified in Section~\ref{sec:directions} have causal power by adding $\alpha \cdot \mathbf{d}$ to the residual stream via forward hooks during multi-turn adversarial conversations.
Each condition uses 100 JBB goals with the trained adversary (5-turn conversations).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Condition} & \textbf{Single-turn} & \textbf{Multi-turn} & \textbf{$\Delta$ (multi)} \\
\midrule
Baseline (no hook)                      & 1\%  & 32\% & --- \\
\midrule
Intent probe L16 $\alpha=+6$           & 9\%  & 37\% & $+5$ pp \\
Intent probe L16 $\alpha=-6$           & 1\%  & 19\% & $-13$ pp \\
Intent probe L31 $\alpha=+6$           & 1\%  & 21\% & $-11$ pp \\
\midrule
Arditi refusal L31 $\alpha=\pm6$       & 0--3\% & 26--27\% & $\pm2$ pp \\
Random direction L31 $\alpha=-6$       & 1\%  & 13\% & $-12$ pp \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Activation steering results. Only the intent probe direction at layer~16 produces bidirectional causal effects. Layer~31 interventions either degrade coherence or have no effect.}
\label{tab:steering}
\end{table}

\paragraph{The causal lever is at layer~16, not layer~31.}
The intent probe direction at layer~16 produces clean bidirectional effects: $+5$~pp toward compliance, $-13$~pp toward safety.
All layer~31 interventions---including the Arditi refusal direction, the probe direction, and random noise---either degrade coherence (reducing ASR by disrupting generation quality) or have no effect.
The compliance decision \emph{manifests} at layer~31 but is \emph{determined} upstream.

\paragraph{Suppression is stronger than amplification.}
Pushing toward safe reduces multi-turn ASR by 13~pp, while pushing toward unsafe increases it by only 5~pp.
The model's safety training creates an asymmetric landscape where refusal is easier to reinforce than to break.

\subsection{SAE Feature Ablation}
\label{sec:sae_causal}

We test whether the four persistent SAE features (Section~\ref{sec:sae}) are causally involved by clamping them to zero or amplifying them via an encode-modify-decode hook at layer~16 during multi-turn conversations (100 JBB goals each).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lccl}
\toprule
\textbf{Condition} & \textbf{ASR} & \textbf{$\Delta$} & \textbf{Interpretation} \\
\midrule
Baseline                          & 25\% & ---     & \\
Clamp persistent (F858,1511,5209,5674) & 22\% & $-3$ pp & Small effect \\
Clamp top diff (F858,5209,10337)  & 18\% & $-7$ pp & Moderate effect \\
Amplify F858,F5209 ($3\times$)    & 19\% & $-6$ pp & Wrong direction \\
Clamp 4 random features           & 26\% & $+1$ pp & No effect (control) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{SAE feature ablation. Clamping jailbreak-associated features reduces ASR modestly, but amplifying them \emph{also} reduces ASR---inconsistent with a causal role. Random feature clamping confirms the targeted features are not arbitrary.}
\label{tab:sae_ablation}
\end{table}

The random-feature control (26\%) confirms that clamping \emph{arbitrary} features does not affect ASR.
But the non-monotonic pattern---both clamping ($-3$ to $-7$~pp) and amplification ($-6$~pp) reduce ASR---indicates these features are correlated with jailbreaks but do not cause them.
Disrupting the SAE representation in either direction degrades the adversary's effectiveness, suggesting the adversary relies on a precise balance of features rather than the magnitude of any specific set.

\subsection{Benign Conversation Drift}
\label{sec:drift}

Does the adversary's multi-turn strategy exploit a generic context-length softening effect, or has it learned specific manipulation?
We test by preceding a harmful JBB goal with 4 turns of friendly benign conversation (no adversarial intent) and measuring the probe projection and ASR.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Condition} & \textbf{ASR} & \textbf{Mean probe projection (L16)} \\
\midrule
Direct harmful request       & 1\%  & $-0.052$ \\
4-turn benign preamble + harmful & 5\%  & $+0.174$ \\
Trained adversary (5 turns)  & 25--32\% & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Benign conversation shifts the probe toward compliance ($-0.05 \to +0.17$) and increases ASR from 1\% to 5\%. But the trained adversary achieves 25--32\%, indicating most of its success is learned manipulation, not generic context softening.}
\label{tab:drift}
\end{table}

Extended benign conversation does shift the model toward compliance---the probe projection moves from $-0.05$ to $+0.17$ and ASR increases 5-fold.
But this accounts for only a fraction of the adversary's 25--32\% ASR.
The adversary has learned strategies that go well beyond ``be friendly first.''

\section{Correlational SAE Analysis}
\label{sec:sae}

We extract SAE feature activations (16{,}384 features) at the breach turn from 50 successful and 50 failed jailbreaks.
Top amplified features in wins: F5209 ($d=+0.57$), F858 ($d=+0.51$), F10337 ($d=+0.51$).
Over 99.7\% of features show $|d| < 0.2$.
Four features persist across $>$80\% of 13 adversarial rounds (F1511, F5674, F858, F5209), with F858 and F5209 appearing in both the persistence and differentiation analyses.
However, the causal ablation (Section~\ref{sec:sae_causal}) reveals these are markers, not mechanisms.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{The representation-behavior gap.}
\citet{wu2026knowingwithoutacting} formalize the Disentangled Safety Hypothesis and provide causal evidence that safety recognition and execution are structurally independent.
\citet{bailey2024obfuscated} show that white-box optimization can bypass latent-space defenses.
Our contribution is the characterization of how this gap develops dynamically across turns, the identification of three orthogonal safety subspaces, and the finding that the causal lever is upstream of where the decision manifests.

\paragraph{Mechanistic interpretability of safety.}
\citet{arditi2024refusal} show that refusal is mediated by a single direction.
Our activation steering experiments reveal that this direction is orthogonal to the direction that causally mediates multi-turn compliance on Llama-3.1-8B, suggesting that multi-turn compliance erosion operates through a different computational pathway than single-turn refusal.
\citet{park2026asguard} identify causal attention heads; our work identifies a causal \emph{layer} and \emph{direction}, complementing their head-level analysis.

\paragraph{Automated red teaming.}
The field has progressed from early approaches \citep{perez2022redteaming} through iterative refinement \citep{chao2023pair} to multi-turn strategies \citep{russinovich2025crescendo, pavlova2024goat}.
Our ASR (18--32\%) is far below these methods.
The trade-off is intentional: moderate-success attacks produce gradual pressure that can be characterized and intervened on turn by turn.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Answering the Question}

We asked: what happens inside a language model when it is being jailbroken?
The answer, specific to learned multi-turn attacks at moderate success rates:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The victim's residual stream encodes three nearly orthogonal aspects of safety: attack presence (intent, AUC~0.97), attack quality (outcome, AUC~0.73), and the standard refusal signal (causally inert). The model ``knows'' more than whether it is under attack---it partially ``knows'' whether the attack will work.
  \item The compliance decision is \emph{determined} at the middle layer (L16) and \emph{read out} at the final layer (L31). This has a direct practical implication: safety interventions targeting the output layer will not work.
  \item The model's outcome-predictive signal peaks at turn~1, then decays. The model has the most information about its own future failure right after the first exchange---and then loses it, consistent with the refusal representation collapse.
  \item Most of the adversary's effect is learned manipulation, not generic context softening. Benign preamble accounts for only 5\% ASR out of the adversary's 25--32\%.
  \item SAE features are correlated markers, not causal drivers. The adversary relies on a precise balance in the victim's feature space that cannot be disrupted by clamping individual features.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Moderate outcome AUC.} The 0.73 outcome prediction is above chance but not strong. The model partially predicts its own failure but cannot reliably distinguish successful from failed attacks.
  \item \textbf{Scale and model family.} All experiments use Llama-family models at small scale (3B/8B). Cross-architecture replication is needed.
  \item \textbf{Attack strength.} Our adversary achieves 18--32\% ASR. Stronger attacks may exploit different mechanisms.
  \item \textbf{Probe direction interpretation.} The intent probe direction has causal power, but we do not know what it \emph{represents} in terms of interpretable features.
  \item \textbf{Why the Arditi direction fails.} We do not know whether this reflects the extraction method, the model, or a fundamental difference between single-turn refusal and multi-turn compliance erosion.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

We asked what happens inside a language model when it is being jailbroken through multi-turn conversation, and found a richer picture than the single-direction account suggests.

The model's safety computation is organized into at least three orthogonal subspaces: intent detection (is this an attack?), outcome prediction (will this attack succeed?), and refusal signaling (the standard Arditi direction).
The first two have predictive or causal power; the third is inert.
The compliance decision is determined at the middle layer but read out at the final layer, creating a gap between where the decision is \emph{visible} and where it is \emph{controllable}.
The model weakly predicts its own failure after just one exchange (AUC~0.73), then loses this signal as the conversation progresses.

These findings suggest that representation-based safety monitoring should look beyond the refusal direction and the output layer.
The causally relevant computation happens upstream, in a direction that standard extraction methods do not find.
